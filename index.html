It's still early days for blockchain

Hamster Dance stage

We are still at a really early stage and the possibilites are largely unwritten. But there are ideas being discussed in a lot of different fields. We're going to look back at how it got started and along the way begin to understand what it is.

IT and business research and advisory firm Gartner charts emerging technologies and assesses where they are in terms of usefuless and viability for business purposes. They do a yearly report of things business leaders should watch, and they chart technologies to identify a sort of lifecycle.

We're leaving the Peak of Inflated Expectations: Early publicity produces a number of success stories — often accompanied by scores of failures. Some companies take action; many do not.

Entering into the Trough of Disillusionment: Interest wanes as experiments and implementations fail to deliver. Producers of the technology shake out or fail. Investments continue only if the surviving providers improve their products to the satisfaction of early adopters.

So we're at this interesting point in the cycle where the craziness is dying down and things are beginning to settle. It's a good time for measured, thoughtful conversations.

Bitcoin

  • Bitcoin is the first application of blockchain technology.
  • It’s a decentralized currency.
  • Peer-to-peer networks, protocols (how those peers talk to each other) and software combine to make it possible to create and use digital currency.

Digital cash is not a new idea

  • Digital cash as a concept goes back to the early 80s, but there were some persistent problems because a bank still has to keep track (it was centralized)
  • Hashcash was a concept introduced in 1997 as a way to thwart email spam (computational puzzle that increased the effort of sending a single email, making spam too computationally expensive) -- this is the Proof of Work concept that blockchain uses.
  • there were other experimental e-cash systems in the 90s.

From a 1964 paper by Paul Baran for the Rand Corporation, "On Distributed Communications: Introduction to Distrubuted Communications Networks"

Blockchain 2.0

Smart contracts

Blocks in the chain begin to be used for more than a transaction record -- beyond just an exchange.

Because you can hold any data in a block, it can also hold code to execute or enforce contracts, and it can do so automatically.

Like a vending machine: you put in your money, make a selection, and you get the candy bar. No negotiation, no variation.

Adds business logic to a transaction. So bitcoin is just the transaction, but the next generation of blockchains introduce the idea of transactions plus conditions.

Cryptographic Concepts

    Hashing

    Digital Signatures

I want to take a small detour and discuss Hashing and Digital Signatures, two main cryptographic concepts that underpin blockchain technology.

Hashing

Using a hashing function or algorithm, *any* input can be represented by a fixed length outcome.

Think of it as a fingerprint or unique identifier.

Cryptographic Hashing

  • Very fast
  • Irreversible
  • No collisions
  • Avalanche effect

should be very fast to compute the hash value for any size or kind of data

it should be impossible to regenerate a message from its hash value (a brute force attack is the only option)

it should avoid hash collisions, each message has its own hash.

if an input is changed slightly (for example, flipping a single bit), the output changes significantly (e.g., half the output bits flip)

Hashing Algorithms

  • Publicly Available Tools
    • MD5
    • Secure Hash Algorithm (SHA)
      • SHA-256
  • Everyday Examples

one of which is SHA-256, currently a common standard, and no matter how big or small your input is, the output will always have a fixed 256-bits length

Peer to Peer Network

When a block is added to the chain, a copy is sent to everyone, or every node, in the network.

You might hear Blockchains referred to as 'trustless'. That term is ambiguous and somewhat inaccurate. The mechanisms in place help all parties in the system can reach a consensus on what the canonical truth is. That distributes or shares trust (and power) among the network’s stakeholders (e.g. developers, miners, and consumers), rather than being concentrated in a single individual or entity such as a bank, government, or financial institution.

Distributed

This model is what makes a blockchain inherently resistant to modification of the data.

Types of Blockchains

  • Public Blockchain
  • Private Blockchain
  • Permissioned Blockchain
  • Consortium Blockchain

public: for the people, by the people and of the people’ Here no one is in charge and anyone can participate -- no single person has the power to validate transactions. can be used when you want your system be truly democratic. Anyone can create smart contracts, transfer money or contribute data. Here users are likely to remain anonymous.

private: a private property of an individual or an organization. still a secure ledger but lacking the accessability and transparency of blockchain as it was imagined.

permissioned: can be public or private, access control mechanisms and distribution can vary.

consortium: a group of companies or representative individuals coming together and making decisions for the best benefit of the whole network.

Real Estate

The World Bank Group reports that 70 percent of the world’s population still lacks access to proper land titling or demarcation.

The status of land rights can affect an individual’s access to economic opportunity

They quote research that shows that "women with secure land rights earn more income, are better able to feed their children and less likely to experience domestic violence. A woman’s right to access, control, transfer, exclude, inherit and otherwise make decisions about land and related resources, improves her livelihood opportunities, her influence over household income and expenditure, and her overall inter household bargaining and decision making power."

Ownsership and transfer of Land and property titles can be handled using blockchain technology.

Identity & Records

The days of fake ids are over!

  • Drivers licenses, identify cards, passports, social security cards, voter registration
  • Instead of carrying cards or keeping paper copies in a fireproof safe, these things can be recorded and verified through a blockchain
  • Proof of insurance, anything notarized; copyrights, patents, trademarks
  • Private records like IOUs and loans, contracts, wills, trusts, anything you sign
